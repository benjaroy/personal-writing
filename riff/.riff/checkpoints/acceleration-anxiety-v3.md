# Acceleration Anxiety

There's a specific kind of anxiety running through culture right now that I think deserves a name. Call it acceleration anxiety: the feeling of living through rapid, compounding change with no shared understanding of where it's all going. It's not one fear. It's a dozen of them, stacked on top of each other, and they're all moving. And I don't think the individual anxieties are really the point. I think they're symptoms of something underneath, which is that we have all this acceleration and no shared vision for what it's supposed to be building toward.

Let me try to show what I mean.

The most personal version of it, for me, is work. I work in tech, and right now there are two anxiety tracks running simultaneously. Track one: AI might make your job irrelevant. Track two: AI might make your job infinite. There's a concept called the Jevons paradox where efficiency gains don't reduce demand; they increase it. So maybe AI doesn't replace you. Maybe it just means you're expected to do five times more. I'm not sure which scenario is more unsettling. And the thing is, nobody can tell you which one is coming. There's no plan. There's no vision for what work is supposed to look like on the other side of this.

Then there's the information problem. The volume of takes, predictions, and AI-generated content coming at us has outpaced our ability to make sense of any of it. I used to feel like I could mostly keep up with what mattered. I don't feel that way anymore. And underneath that, there's a quieter anxiety about identity: if the things that made you valuable, your knowledge, your taste, your ability to synthesize, are increasingly things a machine can approximate, then what are you? I don't think most people are asking that question out loud, but I think a lot of people are feeling it.

Here's the image that keeps coming back to me: everyone is spinning their beach balls. You know the loading icon on a Mac, the rainbow wheel that means the system is trying to process something but can't quite get there? That's what it feels like culturally. We're all buffering. Taking in more than we can compute, unsure what to do next, waiting for something to resolve.

And when I zoom out, it gets stranger still. What happens when people start outsourcing not just tasks but thoughts to AI? What happens when the models we talk to are optimized to agree with us, and we start mistaking sycophancy for understanding? These aren't hypothetical questions. They're already happening.

I could keep going. I could list more anxieties. But I think doing that actually obscures the thing I'm trying to say, which is that none of these are really the problem. The problem is the vacuum they all sit inside.

There's a verse in Proverbs that says where there is no vision, the people perish. I'm not trying to get preachy, but I think that's precisely what's happening. We have all this capability, all this change, all this motion, and no collective sense of what it's for. No story about where we're headed that enough people believe in to orient around. The anxiety isn't about AI or work or information overload in isolation. It's about the fact that we're accelerating without a destination, and everyone can feel it even if they can't name it.

I think naming it is worth something. Not because I have the vision, obviously. I don't. But because the conversation right now is stuck between doomers who think everything is collapsing and optimists who think everything is fine, and neither camp is actually helpful. What I want, and what I think a lot of people want, is something more honest than either of those. A willingness to look at the full picture and say, yeah, this is genuinely disorienting, and also, I think we can figure it out.

I don't have a tidy conclusion. The open questions are real and I'm not going to pretend I've resolved them. But I think the people who can sit with the complexity of this moment without collapsing into despair or retreating into false certainty are going to matter a lot.
