I've been trying to name something that I think a lot of people are feeling right now, and the closest I can get is this: it's not one anxiety. It's a dozen of them, stacked on top of each other, and they're all moving.

There's a layer of uncertainty in the air that feels genuinely new. Not the normal background hum of "life is unpredictable," but something more specific to this moment. The ground is shifting under multiple things at once, and nobody seems to know where it's going. I feel it. I think most people I talk to feel it too, even if they describe it differently.

The most personal version of it, for me, is work. I work in tech, and right now there are two anxiety tracks running simultaneously. Track one: AI might make your job irrelevant. Track two: AI might make your job infinite. There's a concept called the Jevons paradox where efficiency gains don't reduce demand, they increase it. So maybe AI doesn't replace you. Maybe it just means you're expected to do five times more. I'm not sure which scenario is more unsettling.

Then there's the information problem. We're all drowning in takes, predictions, breaking developments, and AI-generated content that's increasingly hard to distinguish from the real thing. The volume of information coming at us has outpaced our ability to make sense of it. I used to feel like I could mostly keep up with what mattered. I don't feel that way anymore.

And underneath all of that, there's a quieter anxiety about status and identity. If the things that made you valuable, your knowledge, your taste, your ability to synthesize, are increasingly things a machine can approximate, then what are you? I don't think most people are asking that question explicitly, but I think a lot of people are feeling it.

Here's the image that keeps coming back to me: everyone is spinning their beach balls. You know the loading icon on a Mac, the rainbow wheel that means the system is trying to process something but can't quite get there? That's what it feels like culturally. We're all buffering. Taking in more than we can compute, unsure what to do next, waiting for something to resolve.

When I zoom out further, the anxiety gets stranger. There's a social fabric version of it: what happens when people start outsourcing not just tasks but thoughts to AI? What happens when the models we talk to are optimized to agree with us, and we start mistaking that for understanding? There's a word for this kind of thing. Sycophancy. And I think we're going to see more of it, not less, as these tools get better at telling us what we want to hear.

Then there's the alignment question, which most people encounter as a vague background dread. Whose values get baked into these models? What does "good" mean when a handful of companies and two or three governments are making decisions that will shape how billions of people interact with intelligence itself? I don't have answers to those questions. I'm not sure anyone does yet.

So what's actually going on here? I've been sitting with all of these threads and I think the root of it is something simpler than any individual anxiety. It's the absence of a shared vision. There's a verse in Proverbs that says where there is no vision, the people perish. I'm not trying to get preachy, but I think that's precisely what's happening. We have all this acceleration, all this capability, all this change, and no collective sense of where we're headed or why. The anxiety isn't really about AI or work or information overload in isolation. It's about the vacuum underneath all of it.

And yet. I'm generally optimistic. Not in a hand-wavy everything-will-be-fine way, but in a more specific sense: I think there is a massive, underserved audience for thoughtful, non-naive hope right now. People are tired of doomers and they're tired of blind optimists. What they want is someone who will look at the full picture honestly and still say, yeah, I think we can figure this out. I want to be that kind of person. I think most people do.

I don't have a tidy conclusion here. The open questions are real and I'm not going to pretend I've resolved them. But I think naming the anxiety is worth something. And I think the people who can sit with the complexity of this moment without collapsing into despair or retreating into false certainty are going to matter a lot in the next few years.
